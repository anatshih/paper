The Impact of Psychological Capital on Work Engagement among Faculty Members at Al-Quds Open University: The Mediating Role of Job Satisfaction

Abstract

[To be written after data analysis is complete][1]

Introduction

Higher education institutions fundamentally depend on the sustained engagement of academic staff to fulfill essential teaching, research, and administrative roles that underpin organizational performance and continuity (Mojado, 2025). Faculty members and academic service personnel, as key contributors to these functions, necessitate a deep understanding of motivational factors to ensure institutional effectiveness and quality assurance (Mojado, 2025). Moreover, recent organizational research emphasizes the strategic cultivation of positive psychological resources, which can enhance employee performance and well-being in dynamic work environments (Luthans et al., 2007). Accordingly, this study examines Psychological Capital (PsyCap) as a developable personal resource and its influence on work engagement among faculty, positing job satisfaction as a critical mediator in this pathway (Avey et al., 2011).

PsyCap represents an individual's positive psychological state of development, characterized by the synergistic integration of hope, optimism, resilience, and self-efficacy (Luthans et al., 2007). Self-efficacy involves confidence in exerting effort toward challenging tasks, optimism entails positive attributions of success over time, hope reflects goal-directed energy with flexible pathways, and resilience denotes the capacity to rebound and grow from adversity (Youssef-Morgan & Luthans, 2015). Rooted in Positive Organizational Behavior (POB), PsyCap focuses on measurable, state-like capacities that organizations can develop to drive performance improvements, distinguishing it from fixed traits through its malleability (Luthans et al., 2007). Empirical meta-analyses confirm PsyCap's strong positive associations with job satisfaction, organizational commitment, citizenship behaviors, and performance, while revealing inverse relationships with turnover intentions, cynicism, stress, and deviance (Avey et al., 2011). Furthermore, utility analyses across diverse samples demonstrate PsyCap's practical value in amplifying positive outcomes and reducing negative ones, with the Psychological Capital Questionnaire (PCQ-24) providing a reliable, stable measure of this construct (Avey et al., 2011; Luthans et al., 2007). These findings suggest PsyCap's potential to foster adaptive responses in demanding academic settings.

In parallel, work engagement has gained prominence as a multifaceted indicator of positive occupational functioning, defined as a positive, fulfilling, work-related state encompassing vigor, dedication, and absorption (Bakker & Demerouti, 2008). Vigor manifests as high energy levels, mental resilience, and persistent effort despite challenges; dedication involves a sense of significance, inspiration, pride, and challenge; and absorption refers to profound immersion in tasks where time perception shifts (Schaufeli et al., 2006). The Utrecht Work Engagement Scale (UWES) operationalizes this model, exhibiting robust psychometric properties and cross-cultural validity (Schaufeli et al., 2006). Synthesizing evidence from meta-analyses, work engagement positively correlates with in-role and extra-role performance, morale, and organizational outcomes, particularly task performance, while aligning closely with attitudinal factors such as job satisfaction and commitment (Christian et al., 2011). However, in higher education contexts, where faculty centrality amplifies the need for sustained motivation, engagement is influenced by leadership and organizational climate, underscoring its relevance for institutional resilience (Mojado, 2025). This study thus integrates PsyCap's resource-building properties with engagement dynamics, particularly in resource-constrained environments like Al-Quds Open University (QOU).

Literature Review and Hypotheses Development

Psychological Capital

Psychological Capital (PsyCap) serves as a higher-order personal resource that amalgamates hope, optimism, resilience, and self-efficacy to promote proactive adaptation and performance in organizational settings (Luthans et al., 2007; Youssef-Morgan & Luthans, 2015). Unlike traditional forms of capital such as human or social resources, PsyCap emphasizes an individual's current positive states and developmental potential, facilitating goal attainment and recovery from setbacks through enhanced self-regulation (Avey et al., 2011). Grounded in Positive Organizational Behavior (POB), PsyCap prioritizes capacities that are state-like—more stable than transient emotions but modifiable through interventions—yielding synergistic effects that exceed the sum of its components, as confirmed by structural analyses validating its integrated structure (Luthans et al., 2007; Youssef-Morgan & Luthans, 2015). This malleability enables organizations to invest in training programs that cultivate PsyCap, contrasting with trait-based approaches by offering actionable pathways to attitudinal and behavioral improvements (Avey et al., 2011).

The PCQ-24 reliably assesses PsyCap across individual and team levels, demonstrating strong internal consistency and test-retest reliability (Luthans et al., 2007). Meta-analytic syntheses reveal PsyCap's consistent positive links to job satisfaction and work engagement, alongside performance and citizenship behaviors, while mitigating negative outcomes like stress and turnover (Avey et al., 2011; Newman et al., 2014). In contrast to isolated component studies, integrated models highlight PsyCap's superior predictive power, suggesting its utility in high-demand contexts where resources buffer demands (Newman et al., 2014). These patterns imply PsyCap's applicability to faculty roles, where psychological strengths can sustain motivation and satisfaction amid challenges, directly informing the current examination of its effects on engagement via satisfaction.

Contextual Background

Palestinian higher education grapples with systemic adversities stemming from occupation-related constraints, including mobility restrictions, infrastructural damage, and chronic underfunding that covers less than 10% of institutional needs, thereby intensifying demands on faculty and disrupting academic continuity (Diab, 2025). Such conditions exacerbate psychological strains, contrasting with more resourced global systems and highlighting the need for personal resources to foster resilience (Diab, 2025). Al-Quds Open University (QOU), as a leader in distance and blended learning, navigates these barriers through innovative modalities, yet encounters additional hurdles like technological deficits, import limitations, and motivational gaps in e-learning adoption (Ghanem & Hamayil, 2011). Faculty training initiatives underscore the role of psychological capacities in pedagogical adaptation, with incentives recommended to enhance engagement and satisfaction (Ghanem & Hamayil, 2011).

QOU's expansive branch network across volatile areas, including Gaza and Hebron, demands comprehensive faculty perspectives to address institution-wide dynamics, where distance education ensures operational continuity but amplifies evaluative pressures on well-being (Diab, 2025). This context extends general engagement models to underrepresented settings, leveraging PsyCap's developability to explain satisfaction-mediated outcomes (Bailey et al., 2017; Youssef-Morgan & Luthans, 2015). By synthesizing resource theories, the study advances theoretical insights into motivational pathways while offering practical implications for faculty support in constrained environments (Avey et al., 2011; Diab, 2025).

PsyCap and Work Engagement

The Job Demands-Resources (JD-R) model positions personal resources like PsyCap as pivotal antecedents to work engagement, particularly in demanding contexts, by aligning its components—efficacy, hope, optimism, and resilience—with vigor, dedication, and absorption to sustain motivational investment (Bakker & Demerouti, 2008; Hakanen & Kaltiainen, 2026). Meta-analyses and longitudinal studies affirm PsyCap's direct positive associations with engagement, where higher levels predict sustained well-being and performance gains, contrasting cross-sectional designs by revealing temporal precedence (Alessandri et al., 2018; Mazzetti et al., 2022). For instance, dynamic models demonstrate that PsyCap growth forecasts engagement elevations that mediate performance improvements, highlighting resource-motivational cascades over static correlations (Alessandri et al., 2018).

These synthesized findings suggest PsyCap equips individuals to channel personal strengths into engaged states, especially for QOU faculty facing e-learning rigors, thereby justifying its anticipated positive impact on engagement.

Based on the above theoretical and empirical evidence, the following hypothesis is proposed:

H1: Psychological capital has a significant positive impact on work engagement among faculty members at Al-Quds Open University. (Alessandri et al., 2018; Mazzetti et al., 2022)

PsyCap and Job Satisfaction

Job satisfaction, a core attitudinal construct in organizational behavior, integrates psychological resources into evaluative frameworks, with PsyCap theory proposing that optimism and self-efficacy generate positive expectancies that enhance overall job fulfillment (Sahoo et al., 2015; Judge et al., 2001). Meta-analytic evidence confirms robust PsyCap-satisfaction linkages, while comparative syntheses across samples reveal consistent positive effects on attitudes, outperforming isolated predictors by encompassing broader developmental dynamics (Avey et al., 2011; Rode et al., 2015). Regression-based studies further validate PsyCap's direct influence, extending beyond correlations to predictive validity in diverse occupational groups (Rode et al., 2015).

In QOU's challenging milieu, where external constraints may undermine satisfaction, PsyCap's role as a buffer implies its capacity to elevate faculty evaluations, supporting its expected positive effect.

H2: Psychological capital has a significant positive impact on job satisfaction among faculty members at Al-Quds Open University. (Rode et al., 2015; Sahoo et al., 2015)

Job Satisfaction and Work Engagement

Job satisfaction and work engagement exhibit strong attitudinal convergence, sharing affective foundations that propel motivational processes and performance (Judge et al., 2001; Christian et al., 2011). Narrative syntheses and meta-analyses highlight their positive interrelations, with satisfaction serving as a precursor that amplifies engagement's vigor and dedication components, though bidirectional influences suggest nuanced dynamics in longitudinal contexts (Bailey et al., 2017; Harter et al., 2002). Empirical regressions affirm satisfaction's direct positive impact on engagement, distinguishing it from mere overlap by evidencing incremental variance in outcomes like productivity (Harter et al., 2002).

For QOU faculty, satisfaction may translate positive job perceptions into heightened engagement amid institutional demands, thus positing a direct facilitative role.

H3: Job satisfaction has a significant positive impact on work engagement among faculty members at Al-Quds Open University. (Bailey et al., 2017; Harter et al., 2002)

Mediating Role of Job Satisfaction

Resource-oriented theories such as JD-R and Conservation of Resources (COR) explain how PsyCap influences engagement through attitudinal mediators like job satisfaction, where personal gains counteract demands to sustain motivational equilibrium (Bakker & Demerouti, 2008; Hobfoll et al., 2018). COR emphasizes satisfaction's amplifying effect amid resource losses, while JD-R frames it as a pathway converting resources into engagement benefits, with meta-analyses confirming partial mediation across studies (Mazzetti et al., 2022; Xanthopoulou et al., 2009). Recent empirical tests using bootstrapping reveal satisfaction's indirect role in the PsyCap-engagement link, though variations in high-demand settings suggest contextual moderation (Hayes, 2018; Rudolph et al., 2017).

In Palestinian higher education's adverse conditions, satisfaction likely strengthens PsyCap's transmission to engagement at QOU, clarifying mechanisms for targeted interventions.

H4: Job satisfaction mediates the relationship between psychological capital and work engagement among faculty members at Al-Quds Open University. (Hayes, 2018; Rudolph et al., 2017)

Methodology

Research Design

This study employs a quantitative, cross-sectional survey design to investigate the direct and indirect effects of Psychological Capital (PsyCap) on work engagement, mediated by job satisfaction, among faculty at Al-Quds Open University (Wolf et al., 2013). Such designs are well-suited for descriptive social science inquiries, enabling the collection of data from a sample at a single time point to estimate relationships and mediation within a cohesive framework, extending beyond simple associations to inferential modeling (Wolf et al., 2013). However, cross-sectional approaches limit causal directionality, warranting longitudinal extensions for future validation (Fong & Ng, 2012).

Data analysis will utilize SPSS (version 28), incorporating descriptive statistics for sample profiling, reliability assessment via Cronbach's alpha, validity evaluation through exploratory factor analysis (EFA) with Kaiser-Meyer-Olkin (KMO) measure and Bartlett's test of sphericity, Pearson correlations for bivariate relations, multiple regression to test direct effects (H1–H3), and the PROCESS macro (Model 4) for mediation analysis of indirect effects (H4) (Hayes, 2018). This regression-based approach accommodates latent constructs measured by multiple items, accounting for measurement error in psychological variables (Wolf et al., 2013). Prior to primary analyses, assumptions will be verified: normality via Shapiro-Wilk tests and Q-Q plots, linearity through scatterplots and residual analyses, and multicollinearity using variance inflation factors (VIF < 5) and tolerance values (> 0.10) (Tabachnick & Fidell, 2019). These steps ensure robust inference in the mediation framework, suitable for the study's scope in educational contexts (Hayes, 2018).

Population and Sample

The target population comprises all full-time faculty members at Al-Quds Open University, aligning with behavioral surveys in higher education that focus on academic staff to capture motivational dynamics (Mojado, 2025; Wolf et al., 2013). Sampling procedures integrate rigorous selection with instrument validation to optimize representativeness and quality (Kelley et al., 2003). Stratified random sampling will be applied to ensure proportional inclusion of subgroups, such as branches and ranks, mitigating bias in non-probability alternatives (Kelley et al., 2003). If logistical constraints arise, a census via institutional email may supplement recruitment (Lindberg et al., 2024).

Sample size determination prioritizes statistical power for mediation, targeting 200–250 responses to achieve ≥ 80% power for detecting medium effects with three predictors, surpassing heuristic rules like the 10-times guideline while avoiding underpowering (Wolf et al., 2013; Soper, 2023). Power calculations via G*Power or similar tools will confirm adequacy for regression and bootstrapping (Faul et al., 2007).

Research Instruments

A self-administered questionnaire will deploy validated multi-item scales to measure PsyCap, job satisfaction, and work engagement, adhering to standards for psychometric rigor in survey research (Kelley et al., 2003). Latent constructs are operationalized with multiple indicators to minimize error and enhance reliability (Wolf et al., 2013).

Data Collection Procedure

The online questionnaire will be distributed via university email and learning platforms, targeting the population or sample with follow-up reminders to maximize response rates (Dillman et al., 2014). Data collection will span 4–6 weeks to preserve cross-sectional integrity while accommodating academic schedules (Kelley et al., 2003). Participation is voluntary, commencing with informed consent outlining study purpose, anonymity, confidentiality, and withdrawal rights (American Psychological Association, 2017).

Ethical Considerations

Ethical protocols prioritize participant autonomy, obtaining explicit consent and ensuring data security through anonymized coding and secure storage (American Psychological Association, 2017). Institutional review board approval will be sought, with debriefing provided post-participation to address any concerns (Wolf et al., 2013).
