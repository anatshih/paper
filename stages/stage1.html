The purpose is to establish a clear profile of the respondents and assess the suitability of the sample for subsequent statistical analyses.

1.3 Detailed Analytical Comment

1.3.1 Gender

The sample comprises 173 males (64.1%) and 97 females (35.9%), indicating a male predominance with a ratio of approximately 1.78:1. Males are overrepresented across all age groups, with the highest concentration in the 41-50 age category (67.0% of that age group). The gender distribution may reflect the institutional staffing composition at QOU.

1.3.2 Age Group

The age distribution is concentrated in the middle-age categories. The 41-50 age group is the largest (88 respondents, 32.6%), followed by 51-60 (80 respondents, 29.6%) and 30-40 (64 respondents, 23.7%). Together, these three categories account for 79.9% of the sample. Respondents aged more than 60 comprise 11.5% (31 respondents), while those under 30 represent only 2.6% (7 respondents). This distribution indicates that the sample is dominated by mid-career and experienced professionals.

1.3.3 Years of Experience at QOU

Experience at QOU is relatively well-distributed across all categories. The 11-15 years category has the highest frequency (67 respondents, 24.8%), followed by 5-10 years (63 respondents, 23.3%) and 16-20 years (53 respondents, 19.6%). Respondents with more than 20 years of experience comprise 18.1% (49 respondents), while those with less than 5 years represent 14.1% (38 respondents). This balanced distribution suggests adequate representation across different tenure levels.

1.3.4 Highest Degree Earned

The sample is highly educated. Master's degree holders constitute the majority at 75.9% (205 respondents), while Bachelor's degree holders represent 19.6% (53 respondents). PhD holders are a small minority at 4.4% (12 respondents). This concentration of advanced degree holders is consistent with the academic context of a higher education institution.

1.3.5 Branch Location

Respondents are distributed across 15 Palestinian branches. The three largest branches are Hebron (38 respondents, 14.1%), Ramallah (37 respondents, 13.7%), and Nablus (36 respondents, 13.3%). Bethlehem contributes 8.1% (22 respondents), while Khan Yunis and Gaza contribute 7.0% and 6.7% respectively. The remaining branches (Tulkarm, Jenin, North Gaza, Jericho, Qalqilya, Tubas, Rafah, and Deir al-Balah) each contribute between 3.3% and 6.3% of the sample. This geographic distribution reflects the organizational structure of QOU across Palestinian territories.

1.5 Summary of Demographic Characteristics

The sample consists of 270 respondents with complete data across all demographic variables. The sample is predominantly male (64.1%), concentrated in the 41-60 age range (62.2%), and highly educated with 75.9% holding Master's degrees. Institutional experience is well-distributed across all tenure levels. Respondents are distributed across 15 Palestinian branches, with the largest concentrations in Hebron, Ramallah, and Nablus. These characteristics indicate a mature, experienced, and academically qualified sample suitable for investigating the research questions.

1.6 Key Observations

  • Sample size (N = 270) is adequate for conducting robust statistical analyses.
  • No missing data across any demographic variable, indicating high data quality.
  • Male predominance (64.1%) is consistent across all age groups and experience levels.
  • Age concentration in 41-60 range (62.2%) indicates sample represents mid-career professionals.
  • Institutional experience is well-balanced across all tenure levels, enhancing representativeness.
  • High educational attainment (75.9% Master's degrees) strengthens credibility of self-reported measures.
  • Geographic representation across 15 branches supports sample diversity.
  • Crosstab analyses confirm logical consistency across demographic variables.

1.7 Implications for Statistical Analysis

The large sample size and complete data support the robustness of inferential testing. The balanced distribution of institutional experience enhances representativeness across tenure levels. However, the gender imbalance and concentration in the 41-60 age range indicate that findings are most applicable to experienced, mid-career professionals. Interpretations of gender-related findings should be made with consideration of the observed gender distribution. The high educational level is a strength, as it increases the likelihood of accurate responses to complex survey items.

1.8 What Did I Learn?

The demographic analysis confirms that the dataset is complete and suitable for advanced statistical analyses. The sample demonstrates adequate size with no missing values, indicating rigorous data collection. The balanced distribution of institutional experience and geographic representation across 15 branches provides a comprehensive organizational perspective. The identified demographic patterns—male predominance and age concentration—establish important context for interpreting subsequent findings and identifying potential limitations in generalizability. Overall, the sample provides a solid basis for investigating the relationships among psychological capital, job satisfaction, and work engagement.

The next stage involves conducting descriptive statistical analysis of the main study variables. This will include examining means, standard deviations, and distribution patterns to establish a deeper understanding of the data and prepare for subsequent inferential analysis.
